The Baseline Problem

Fitness Apparel Brand is a DTC fitness apparel brand — activewear, joggers, hoodies, starter packs. The audit baseline (December 2024 – January 2025) showed a 1.6% overall CVR, an Add-to-Cart rate of 13% (against a 16–20% benchmark for this category), and a desktop bounce rate over 60%.

Before building a test roadmap, the audit identified three distinct problems: a UI layer with concrete broken elements, an attribution layer with a serious measurement conflict, and a channel mix that was funded in the wrong direction.

The UI Problems Suppressing CVR

Broken UI doesn't generate a hypothesis — it generates noise. Every test run on top of broken elements is measuring friction that shouldn't exist. These were documented first and flagged as "just do it" fixes before any A/B tests shipped.

Desktop · 800px+
Images overpower text elements at all desktop sizes starting from 800px — readability broken, hierarchy destroyed. Bounce rate >60% on desktop.
Collection Page
Filter — no response on click, desktop and mobile. Users can't narrow the catalog. Discovery dead-end.
Product Page · Desktop
Sticky quantity button broken — doesn't work, layout incorrect. The primary purchase action on desktop is non-functional.
Homepage · Desktop
Active dropdown doesn't close when another opens. Hover interaction has high latency. Navigation feels broken.
Product Page · Mobile
Image next arrows overlay the size selector — wrong margin. Users trying to select size accidentally swipe images.
All Pages
Quick View doesn't lock background scroll. Page jumps while modal is open — disorienting on mobile.
The principle: These aren't A/B test candidates. They're table stakes. Running CVR optimization on a product page with a non-functional quantity button is like diagnosing a slow engine while the fuel line is disconnected. Fix the broken elements first. Then optimize.

The Attribution Conflict

The more significant finding wasn't in the UI — it was in the data. During the February sale period, Meta Ads Manager reported 123 conversions. GA4 reported 28. The gap is 4×.

Three probable causes identified: UTMs not consistently applied to ad URLs (so Meta gets credit for sessions that arrive untagged), Meta's attribution window (7-day click, 1-day view) counting conversions that happened organically or via email, and GA4's last-click model stripping credit from Meta entirely.

The practical consequence: if you use Meta's number to make budget decisions, you're optimizing an ad channel that is performing 4× worse than it appears. In the same period, email campaigns — which received a fraction of the budget — were converting at 7–10%, while Meta paid traffic sat at 2.34%.

The Sale Event — What the Funnel Could Actually Do

The February 10–17 sale event provided a controlled window to read the real ceiling. With the same broken UI in place (bugs not yet fixed), simply increasing purchase intent through a sale event showed exactly how much headroom the brand had.

The visitor count barely moved (+7%). The revenue moved +45%. That gap is entirely explained by conversion rate and order size — both of which responded to intent-signal changes, not traffic increases. The funnel had the capacity. The baseline measurement and budget allocation were the constraints.

The lesson about attribution: The question is never just "is the channel converting?" It's "which measurement system is right, and what decision am I making based on the wrong one?" Meta's 123 vs GA4's 28 isn't a rounding error — it's a strategic budget misdirection that was costing the brand every month.

What This Engagement Taught Me

  • Attribution conflicts are budget problems, not just data problems. If Meta shows 4× more conversions than GA4, the ad spend is being justified by a number that isn't real. Fixing the UTM discipline and agreeing on a single measurement source is the most valuable thing you can do before running any paid campaign optimization.
  • Email's conversion rate is the benchmark everything else should be measured against. 7–10% on email vs 2.34% on Meta paid isn't a case for cutting Meta — it's a case for understanding what makes email buyers different and replicating those conditions upstream.
  • Broken UI floors CVR in ways that no test can overcome. The quantity button not working on desktop is not a test candidate. It's a revenue leak that blocks every other optimization from registering.
  • Sale events are diagnostic tools. The sale didn't just generate revenue — it revealed the actual conversion ceiling, which was nearly 60% higher than the baseline. That gap is the optimization opportunity.
