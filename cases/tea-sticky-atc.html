When the ATC Follows the Buyer

A premium tea retailer with a long-tail catalog — loose-leaf blends, single-origin selections, brewing kits, subscription boxes. High session duration, healthy Add-to-Cart activity, and a conversion rate that lagged what every engagement signal said was possible. The problem wasn't persuasion. It was navigation — buyers had decided to purchase, then had to scroll five viewport-heights backward to do it.

Phase 1: The Category Most Frameworks Get Wrong

Premium tea is not a commodity e-commerce category. Buyers do not arrive with a specific SKU in mind, add it to cart, and check out. They arrive to learn about a tea — its origin, its flavor profile, its brewing requirements, its caffeine content — and to decide whether it is worth buying. The Product Detail Page (PDP) is a research surface before it is a transaction surface.

This is the buyer behavior:

  • Enters the PDP from a category page, a blog post, a newsletter link, or organic search on the tea's name
  • Reads the description — flavor notes, origin, harvest date, caffeine level — often all of it
  • Scrolls to brewing instructions — water temperature, steep time, ratio
  • Reads customer reviews — particularly the ones mentioning comparable teas they have bought before
  • Returns to the price and package selection — and then realizes the Add-to-Cart button is five scroll-heights above

The standard Conversion Rate Optimization (CRO) playbook says "put the Add-to-Cart button above the fold." That rule works for categories where the buyer has already decided before arriving. For browse-heavy content categories — specialty food, wine, fragrance, premium coffee, tea — the buyer has not decided when they arrive. They decide after reading.

The ATC above the fold is there when it isn't needed. It is missing when it is.

Phase 2: Measuring the Gap

Session recording analysis across ~200 mobile PDP sessions surfaced a consistent behavior pattern. Median scroll depth at the moment of purchase decision — the point where the user stopped reading, started scrolling upward, and looked for the buy action — was deep into the content stack.

The friction is not hesitation. The buyer has decided. The friction is the ~70-point scroll gap between the decision and the button that completes it. On mobile that is five to six full viewports of scrolling backward — enough to lose the buyer to a distraction, a context switch, or a different tab.

The primary Add-to-Cart did not move. A secondary bottom-anchored bar appeared once the primary button scrolled out of view — persistent, compact, tappable without reaching. The buyer who finished reading the reviews no longer had to navigate back to buy.

Why this isn't a trend-chase. Sticky buy boxes are not a design fashion choice. They are a structural response to how mobile users read a product page. The buyer who has just finished 2,000 words of brewing instructions is the most qualified buyer on the page. Asking them to scroll back up to purchase is the most expensive thing you can ask them to do — and it is exactly what the default PDP template asks.

Phase 3: Two Tests, Two Intents

Two tests launched in parallel. They targeted different parts of the buyer journey and were scored against different primary metrics — because optimizing for a single metric would have hidden the value of either test.

Test A · Conversion

Sticky Mobile Add-to-Cart

Persistent bottom-anchored CTA appearing after the primary button scrolls out of view. Compact bar — product title, price, variant selector, prominent tappable action. No overlay, no interruption, no modal.

Primary metric: PDP-to-purchase Conversion Rate on mobile
Test B · Top of Funnel

Subscription Landing Restructure

The subscription-product landing page reorganized to lead with the value proposition (personalized monthly delivery, trial flexibility, curation quality) rather than the product catalog. Subscription-intent traffic was being qualified poorly by the product-first layout.

Primary metric: Subscription landing page visits reaching meaningful engagement (scroll, click, session duration)

Before: Standard PDP — user scrolls past flavor notes, brewing guide, ingredients, reviews. Original ATC button is 5+ viewport-heights above. No way to purchase without scrolling back.
After: Persistent sticky bar at bottom of viewport — product name, price, "ADD TO CART" always visible regardless of scroll depth. +43.28% CVR lift on mobile.

Reading the +318%. A 318% lift in landing page visits is not a 318% lift in subscription conversions. Subscription has a longer sales cycle — buyers research over multiple sessions, compare against other services, and commit after consideration rather than on first touch. What this result proved was that the top of the subscription funnel was finally targeting the right intent. The conversion lift is measurable only after the full sales cycle plays out. Reporting an early-indicator metric as the win, and explicitly marking the downstream metrics as pending, is how a test in a subscription category should be communicated to executives.

Phase 5: Why This Category Breaks Standard CRO Advice

Most CRO playbooks say:

  • "Put the Add-to-Cart above the fold."
  • "Reduce the number of scrollable sections on the PDP."
  • "Shorten your description copy."
  • "Never hide the price."

In a commodity e-commerce category — where the buyer arrived already knowing what they wanted — these rules are correct. In premium tea, premium coffee, specialty food, fragrance, and subscription content categories, each rule is either wrong or incomplete:

  • "ATC above the fold" — incomplete. The ATC must be above the fold and reachable wherever the buyer currently is. One static position is not enough when the decision happens at depth.
  • "Reduce scrollable sections" — wrong. The sections are the product in this category. Removing them strips the persuasion. The intervention is making the buy action keep up with the sections, not removing the sections.
  • "Shorten descriptions" — wrong. Description depth is how the buyer qualifies the product. Customers actively want the flavor notes, the origin, the brewing guide. Shortening them undercuts the decision.
  • "Never hide the price" — correct, but add: never hide the buy action either. Visible price + unreachable buy action is the same category of mistake as invisible price + visible buy action. Both break the path from decision to purchase.

What This Engagement Taught Me

  • Category determines playbook, not the other way around. CRO rules that are stated as universal are almost always universal for a single category and wrong for the next one. The first question every engagement answers is what kind of buyer is this, and how do they read a product page — not what's our test idea.
  • Sticky buy boxes are structural, not stylistic. A sticky ATC is the visible representation of "the buy action must follow the buyer." In content-heavy categories this is a revenue intervention. In short-decision categories it is visual clutter. Both statements are true at the same time.
  • Subscription tests read differently than conversion tests. A 318% top-of-funnel lift in a subscription category is a leading indicator, not a lagging one. The readout must explicitly mark downstream metrics as pending — otherwise stakeholders either over-celebrate the early signal or under-weight the real conversion outcome once it lands.
  • Desktop parity is a win signal, not a problem. When a mobile-specific intervention does not move desktop, that is evidence the intervention is well-scoped. Aggregate-level metric flatness would be a problem. Device-segmented flatness on the device the intervention did not target is exactly what the hypothesis predicted.
  • The buyer who finished reading is the highest-value buyer on the page. They self-selected through the entire persuasion stack. Making them scroll back to buy is the costliest friction in the funnel — because it is applied to the buyer most likely to convert.
