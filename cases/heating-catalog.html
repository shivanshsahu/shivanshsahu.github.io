The Revenue the Dashboard Was Hiding

A US-based retailer in home heating and outdoor living — deep catalog, two distinct buyer populations (contractors specifying commercial installations, homeowners replacing a unit). Both landing on the same collection pages, both receiving the same thin information. I ran two parallel tests — Collection and PDP. The PDP test read flat. The Collection test generated $391K/mo in incremental revenue. The story: the PDP wasn't leaking. The Collection grid was.

Phase 1: Where Everyone Wants to Test (and Why They're Wrong)

When a catalog retailer has healthy traffic and underperforming conversion, the instinctive test target is the Product Detail Page (PDP). It's where "conversion happens." It's the page with the Add-to-Cart button. Every stakeholder in the room wants to test the PDP.

Here the PDP had a reasonable conversion rate for buyers who reached it. Session recordings showed engagement on the product page, not abandonment. The heatmaps weren't alarming. The funnel math said the PDP was converting at the expected rate for the category.

The Collection page was a different story. Session recordings showed a repeatable pattern: users landing on a collection page, scanning the grid for three to four seconds, then bouncing — not to a competitor's cart, but backwards into search results. They weren't rejecting the brand. They were rejecting the information architecture. The grid was not telling them enough to decide whether clicking through was worth it.

The diagnostic hypothesis. The collection page was failing to qualify buyers well enough for the right ones to advance. The PDP was fine at converting qualified traffic — but it was receiving under-qualified traffic. A PDP test would have improved a page that didn't need improving. A Collection test would improve the page that was actually leaking.

Five fields added to the collection card:

  • BTU rating — the single most-searched specification in this category
  • Coverage area (sq ft) — what contractors and homeowners actually measure against
  • Fuel compatibility — propane, natural gas, electric — binary pre-qualifier
  • Venting type — direct-vent, vent-free, flue-required
  • Application tag — residential, commercial, or both

These were not new specifications. They had always existed — buried inside the PDP's description tab. Elevating them onto the card let the buyer decide on the grid whether a product was worth clicking through.

Phase 3: Two Parallel Tests

Rather than guess which surface to test, I scoped and ran both in parallel. A/A-verified split on each, non-overlapping traffic, read independently.

Running both surfaces in parallel proved exactly the opposite: CRO was working, the team had just been pointing it at the wrong page.

The +6% chatbot engagement uplift was a secondary finding — buyers who now had the qualifying information on the card were more likely to ask follow-up questions via chat, feeding better data back to customer service and reducing pre-sales support load.

The PDP test was not a failure

Worth saying explicitly: the PDP test reading flat was the most valuable single data point in the program. It proved the conversion problem was not on the PDP. Without that flat read, the team would have continued scoping PDP tests for the next quarter, with predictable results. The flat PDP read redirected development bandwidth toward structural work upstream — where the actual leak was.

What This Engagement Taught Me

  • The Collection page is the real conversion surface for specs-driven catalogs. In B2B and technical e-commerce where buyers arrive with measurable requirements, they decide on the grid. The PDP is confirmation, not persuasion. Most programs test the PDP because that's what general-audience CRO articles prescribe — and miss the surface where the actual qualification is happening.
  • Run parallel tests when the diagnosis is ambiguous. The Collection vs PDP test saved the program from scoping the next three PDP tests. Running both at once cost marginal additional effort and produced definitive routing information for the rest of the roadmap.
  • A flat test result is diagnostic, not a failure. The PDP test reading flat was informationally positive — it eliminated an entire test category from future scoping. Programs that treat flat results as failures are programs that don't learn.
  • The specifications the buyer searches for should live wherever the buyer makes the decision. The BTU rating had always been on the PDP. Moving it to the grid wasn't adding data — it was moving data to the decision point. Same information, different location, dramatically different outcome.
  • Secondary metrics (chatbot engagement) signal whether the real problem was solved. A test that lifts primary CVR but doesn't move secondary signals might be a surface-level intervention. A test that lifts CVR and also moves chat engagement, return-visitor rate, and support ticket volume is a structural intervention. This was the latter.
