The Products Nobody Understood But Everybody Bought

80% of visitors left without adding anything. 70% left within 10 seconds. And yet 92% of orders came from bundle pack deals — a product format that the site made confusing to find and harder to build. The opportunity wasn't a conversion rate problem. It was a discovery and clarity gap between what was driving revenue and how the site surfaced it.

The Brand and the Data Period

An outdoor accessories brand selling silicone rings, face shields, straw hats, hoodies, and bundle packs — products primarily for fishing, hunting, and outdoor work, with a strong patriotic theme. Facebook paid drove 43% of sessions and 37% of revenue. Google CPC contributed 14.6% of traffic and 18% of revenue. The audit period ran September 2022 through February 2023.

The Paradox

The Contradiction That Defined the Diagnosis
Session RecordingsUsers are not reading product descriptions
vs
Customer Survey"A proper description is an important aspect for a visitor to purchase products"

Users weren't skipping descriptions because they didn't want information. They were skipping because dense paragraph text wasn't scannable. The information they needed to buy was on the page in a format they wouldn't use. Replace paragraphs with icon rows — activity categories, material badges, weather suitability — and the same information becomes decision-ready.

What Competitors Did That the Brand Didn't

The competitor review covered Columbia, Guy Harvey, Pelagic, and Salt Life. Four patterns appeared repeatedly that were absent from the brand's pages:

Columbia
Auto-scrolling announcement bar showing all offers. Product finder quiz on PLP. Upsell items embedded between product detail sections. Multiple CTAs on homepage hero.
Guy Harvey
Storytelling copy in hero. Featured product directly below hero. "Review highlights" section below product details — trust building close to the buy decision.
Pelagic
Clear single UVP on hero. Visual filter on PLP (not hidden in a drawer). Checkout step indicators. Single featured category section per page.
Salt Life
UGC with offers — social proof + commercial in same module. Variant selection exposed on PLP (no PDP click needed). Optimized drawer cart with Amazon checkout option.

The Test Roadmap

  • Promote packs to first-fold homepage: 92% of revenue from packs. Best sellers section moved up; pack deals given dedicated hero placement. Stop routing traffic to individual product categories first.
  • Hamburger menu simplification: 35% of all homepage taps went to the hamburger menu. Restructure with visual category tiles — pack deals reachable in one tap, not through nested menus.
  • Pack builder UX: Add step-by-step progress prompts, visible "items in your pack" summary, clear "Next" button with visual affordance. User testing confirmed: most users abandoned after selecting 1–2 items because they didn't know what to do next.
  • Icon-first product benefits: Replace paragraph descriptions with scannable icon rows — activity categories (fishing, hunting, work), material properties, weather suitability. Users didn't want to read. Give them a format they'd actually use to make a decision.
  • Cart and checkout assurance: No trust signals at cart or checkout. Guarantee callout, free shipping threshold, return policy clarity — the final hesitations that break buyers at the last step.
  • Review visibility: Reviews received strong attention in heatmaps but were placed too low on collection and product pages. Surface stars and review count at the card level on PLPs. Move review highlights above product description on PDPs.
The insight that carries: A brand with strong post-purchase satisfaction, repeat buyers, and favorable comparisons against major competitors was leaking 80% of its traffic because the site couldn't efficiently route visitors to the product format they wanted. The product was right. The path to it was broken.
