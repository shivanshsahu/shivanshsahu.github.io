The Products Nobody Understood But Everybody Bought

80% of visitors left without adding a single item to cart. 70% left within 10 seconds. And yet 92% of orders came from bundle pack deals — a product format that the site made confusing to find and harder to build. The opportunity wasn't in the conversion rate. It was in the discovery gap between what was driving revenue and how prominently the site surfaced it.

The Brand

Outdoor Accessories Brand ([brand].com) sells outdoor accessories — silicone rings, face shields, straw hats, hoodies, and bundle packs designed for fishing, hunting, and outdoor work. It's a patriotic-theme brand with strong customer satisfaction, good reviews, and a product that buyers love after they receive it.

The audit period ran September 2022 to February 2023. Facebook paid drove 43% of sessions and 37% of revenue. Google CPC contributed 14.6% of traffic and 18% of revenue. The brand had traffic. The problem was what happened to it.

The Paradox

The most important finding in this engagement wasn't in the heatmaps or the funnel data — it was in the contradiction between what the data showed and what the surveys said.

The Contradiction That Defined the Diagnosis

Customer SurveyA proper description is an important aspect for a visitor to purchase

Users weren't skipping descriptions because they didn't want information. They were skipping descriptions because the format wasn't scannable — dense paragraphs where they expected icons, bullet benefits, and visual proof. The information they needed to buy was on the page. The format made it invisible.

The funnel looked like a typical traffic quality problem. But the data told a more specific story: pack deal pages had the lowest drop-offs of any page on the site. Summer clearance pages led to 31% checkout in first interaction. The product format that converted best was the one with the most friction to discover and build.

The Pack Deal Gap

92% of orders came from pack deals. The homepage surfaced individual product categories first. The hamburger menu — which 35% of taps went to — was the primary navigation tool, and users used it specifically to find pack deals rather than scroll the homepage. The site was routing traffic to its weakest converting products first.

The navigation heatmap read: Hamburger menu = 4,807 taps (35% of all taps). Right arrow on Spring Best Sellers carousel = 1,932 taps (14%). The hero banner = 828 taps (6%). Users weren't engaging with the content the homepage was promoting. They were navigating around it to find what they actually wanted — which was packs.

The pack builder problem

Once users found the pack builder, a second friction point emerged. User testing confirmed it directly: "They did not know they need to click 'Next.' Users also wanted to customize their packs. Most are abandoning the builder page after selecting 1–2 items." The pack builder — the highest-converting product format on the site — had no clear process explanation, no step indicators, and no prompts.

The satisfaction reviews and the hesitation surveys were about completely different aspects of the product. Satisfied buyers talked about quality, comfort, and value. Hesitant buyers needed size information, material details, and durability reassurance — none of which the product pages made easy to find.

The Test Roadmap

The audit produced a prioritized list of changes, starting with the highest-leverage items:

  • Promote packs to first-fold homepage: If 92% of revenue comes from packs, the homepage should lead with packs — not categories. Best sellers section moved up; pack deals given dedicated hero placement.
  • Hamburger menu simplification: Restructure the navigation with visual category tiles so users can navigate to pack deals in one tap, not through a nested menu requiring three.
  • Pack builder UX: Add step-by-step prompts, progress indicator, and visible "what you're building" summary at the top. The builder needed to feel guided, not abandoned.
  • Icon-first product benefits: Replace dense description paragraphs with scannable icon rows — activity categories (fishing, hunting, work), material badges, weather suitability. Users didn't want to read. Give them a format they'd actually use.
  • Cart assurance layer: No trust signals at cart or checkout. Added guarantee callout, free shipping threshold, and return policy — all the reassurances that reduce the final hesitation before purchase.
