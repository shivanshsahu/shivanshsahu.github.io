Why the Diagnostic Comes First

A CRO program without a prior diagnostic is a random walk through the hypothesis space. You test things that look like good ideas — "add social proof," "improve the hero," "simplify checkout" — and you get results that don't compound because each test answers a different question about a different problem with no connecting logic.

The diagnostic phase answers one question before any test is scoped: where is revenue leaking, and why? Once that's established, the test roadmap isn't a list of ideas. It's an ordered queue of answers to a specific question.

The Audit — 7 Workstreams

01
Analytics Audit
GA4 event accuracy, data cleanliness, funnel completeness. Can the data be trusted before it's read?
02
Traffic Source Analysis
Which channels drive sessions vs conversions. Which are sending buyers vs browsers.
03
Funnel Analysis
Landing → product → cart → checkout → purchase. Step-by-step drop-off sizing and source attribution.
04
Cart & Checkout
Abandonment at each checkout step. Payment exits. Friction between ATC and completed purchase.
05
Qualitative Analysis
Heuristic evaluation, session recordings, heatmaps, scroll maps, customer journey mapping.
06
VoC Surveys
On-site pre-purchase, post-purchase, exit-intent. What buyers needed and what stopped those who didn't convert.
07
Review Mining
Sentiment analysis, theme extraction, buying triggers, conversion barriers from existing customer language.
Key signal from the diagnostic: Search was the highest-clicked element on the site at 3.8% CTR. Users who reached the search results page converted at 8% — 70% above the site average CVR. That's a data-surfaced roadmap priority, not a best-practice assumption.

The Tests

Winner · Deployed on Desktop
Sticky Header Bar
Sitewide · Mobile + Desktop · Sep 11 – Oct 16, 2025
Hypothesis
If the header becomes sticky after initial scroll, users will find products faster — less pogo-sticking, higher PDP visits, cart opens, and conversion.
Result
Desktop performed strongly. Mobile declined — the sticky header competed with the existing sticky Add-to-Cart button. Two persistent sticky elements on a narrow screen hurt ATC on mobile. Decision: desktop 100% deployment, mobile reverted to control.

Winner · Device-Forked Deployment
Variant Chips vs Dropdown
Product Detail Page · Mobile + Desktop · Sep 22 – Oct 8, 2025
Hypothesis
Displaying product variants as visual chips instead of a dropdown will increase ATC by reducing friction. Session recordings showed users interacting with the dropdown but ignoring icon navigation and jumping to the comparison chart — a sign of confusion in the variant selection flow.
Result — Chips Won on Desktop. Dropdown Won on Mobile.

Loss · Reverted
PDP Value Proposition Block
Product Detail Page · Mobile + Desktop · Sep 29 – Oct 16, 2025
Hypothesis
A value proposition block below the ATC button — surfacing benefit copy to address hesitations — will increase ATC and CVR.
Result
No meaningful impact on ATC or CVR. The hesitations being addressed weren't resolved by copy alone — this is a trust signal problem, not a copy problem. The next iteration (COA badge prominence + lab verification placement above the fold) was scoped directly from this learning.

The Device Fork — Why Aggregate Results Mislead

The variant chips test is the clearest example of why you never read a result at the topline aggregate. Combine mobile and desktop and the two variants move in different directions — the aggregate is noise. The signal is only visible in the segment split.

A team reading aggregate results would have deployed chips everywhere and lost money on mobile. A team reading segment results deploys chips on desktop and dropdown on mobile — capturing the lift on both surfaces. That's the difference between a $48K win and a costly rollback.

The standard: Every test with a meaningful mobile/desktop split should be segmented before any deployment decision. The aggregate CVR is the number that obscures. The segment CVR is the number that tells you what to ship.
