The Survey Question Nobody Was Asking

A US-based B2B LED lighting distributor with healthy traffic, competitive pricing, and a conversion rate that underperformed what every quantitative signal suggested was possible. No amount of dashboard analysis pointed at a specific villain. I ran two qualitative instruments in the same window — 200 Clarity sessions and a live on-site survey — and a single visitor response reframed the entire test program: "What's the difference between T8 and T12 bulbs?"

Phase 1: The Silent Underperformance

The brand sold LED lighting into a specific buyer profile — contractors, facility managers, and commercial installers — who did not browse the site. They arrived with a spec in mind: T8 tube, 4ft, 40W-equivalent, 5000K color temperature, daylight white. They wanted to validate the product matched that spec, confirm it was in stock, see the price, and check out.

Traffic quality was strong. Paid acquisition was running efficiently. Page load times were acceptable. The site design was functional and clean. By every quantitative measure, the conversion rate should have been higher than it was.

A standard Conversion Rate Optimization (CRO) audit surfaces the leak through funnel analysis, heatmaps, and competitive benchmarking. I ran all three. None of them pointed at a specific villain. The funnel drop-off was distributed — not concentrated on a single stage. Heatmaps showed engagement across the PDP, not abandonment at a specific element. The design was comparable to direct competitors.

When the quantitative audit doesn't point at a specific failure, the answer is never to test harder. It's to change instruments.

Phase 2: Two Instruments, Same Window

I deployed two qualitative research programs in parallel:

  • 200 Microsoft Clarity session recordings — sampled across device, entry page, and exit behavior. Not watched one-by-one. Coded systematically by behavior pattern (spec-hunter / browser / comparison-shopper / bounce), device context, and exit-triggering behavior.
  • A persistent on-site visitor survey on every Product Detail Page (PDP): "What additional information would help you feel more confident completing your purchase today?" Deliberately open-ended. Deliberately phrased around confidence rather than design or experience.
Methodology note. A heatmap answers where users stopped. A survey answers why. Running them in the same window — overlapping date ranges, same PDP population — means every behavioral pattern you identify in Clarity has a stated-reason answer waiting for it in the survey. Running them sequentially doubles the interpretation time because each instrument has to be analyzed against its own memory of what the other instrument showed last month.

Phase 4: The Device Divergence

While the survey was running, the 200 Clarity recordings were being coded. The pattern that surfaced was not the one the quantitative funnel had suggested: the site had two distinct buyer populations with fundamentally different reading behaviors, and they needed to be tested as two separate experiences.

Desktop — The Scanner

Session durations averaged longer. Users moved between product tabs, used comparison behavior, opened multiple tabs in the same session, and cross-referenced specifications against notes or external sites.

  • Horizontal viewport allowed spec tables to coexist with the Add-to-Cart button
  • Below-the-fold content was read by the majority of sessions that reached the PDP
  • Comparison behavior dominated — this buyer needed structured specification data, not just more of it

Mobile — The Fast Searcher

Session durations were shorter and more fragmented. Users arrived from search with high intent, evaluated the page in under a minute, and either added to cart or bounced.

  • Content below the first fold had approximately 80% drop-off — functionally invisible to most mobile sessions
  • Any spec table placed below the image carousel was adding page weight without adding visibility
  • The single most important question — "does this meet my spec" — had to be answerable in the first viewport

A single "responsive" PDP template couldn't serve both populations. The mobile experience needed compression; the desktop experience needed structured expansion. The test architecture forked.

Phase 5: The Tests

Three coordinated tests launched on the PDP. Each was device-scoped — traffic was split by viewport width, not unified into an aggregate A/B.

Test 1 — Mobile First-Fold Spec Summary

The three most-searched specifications — lumens output, wattage equivalent, color temperature — moved directly below the product title as a compact inline summary. The below-the-fold specification table was removed entirely. Page weight dropped; first-fold information density rose.

Test 2 — Desktop Spec Comparison Block

The desktop variant introduced an expandable comparison block in the first fold, letting buyers see the current product's core specifications alongside two commonly compared alternatives from the same catalog. Structured comparison, not more text.

Test 3 — Tab Navigation Reorder

The PDP tab navigation — Description / Specifications / Reviews — reordered to lead with Specifications. The buyer's first question was never "tell me about this product." It was "does this match the spec I came here looking for." The default tab should answer the first question.

Baseline: control PDP traffic on the unmodified template during the same 4-week test window, 50/50 device-segmented split allocation. Significance reached within 2 weeks on mobile; desktop required a longer window due to lower session volume.

The return-visitor lift was the most interesting secondary finding. Visitors who had previously viewed the PDP without purchasing were returning and converting at a higher rate on the variant. The interpretation: specification clarity wasn't just closing first-time sessions — it was reducing the research friction that was sending buyers back to search results to verify the product elsewhere between visits.

Full Program: Test-by-Test Results

This engagement ran 15 tests across PDP, homepage, collection, cart, and sitewide surfaces. Seven won. Four lost. Four were flat or inconclusive. The full ledger:

The losses mattered too: The Homepage Optimization test confirmed the existing layout worked. The Certifications Display showed that credentials alone don't answer spec questions. The Quick View + Infinity Scroll loss proved B2B buyers in this category prefer pagination over infinite scroll — they want control over product comparison, not speed. Every loss pre-qualified the next test.

What This Engagement Taught Me

  • When the quantitative audit doesn't point at a villain, change instruments. The funnel, the heatmaps, and the benchmark analysis all said "this site is fine." The qualitative instruments said otherwise. A site that looks fine in aggregate and converts below expectation is almost always losing buyers to a problem no quantitative tool will surface on its own.
  • Run a heatmap and a survey in the same window, not sequentially. Every behavioral pattern has a stated-reason waiting for it in the survey data. Running them in series means you're analyzing one instrument against a stale memory of the other.
  • Ask the question you're not biased toward. "What would help you feel more confident completing your purchase today?" is not the same question as "is there anything wrong with this page?" The first surfaces specification anxiety. The second surfaces design complaints.
  • Device isn't a modifier — it's a buyer identity. Mobile and desktop visitors on this site were two different populations with two different reading strategies. Treating them as a single population with a responsive template ignored the structural split.
  • One real sentence from a real customer is worth a week of dashboard interpretation. The T8/T12 question was not the insight. It was the moment the theme became visible. The insight was the distribution — that 42% of buyers were asking questions the PDP could answer, if the PDP were built for the buyer's decision instead of the merchandiser's narrative.
